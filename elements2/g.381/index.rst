G.381
=====

**Heath ID:** :ref:`X.36`

   If two rational straight lines commensurable in square only be added together, the whole is irrational; and let it be called
          binomial.

For let two rational straight lines AB, BC commensurable in square only be added together;

I say that the whole AC is irrational.

For, since AB is incommensurable in length with BCâ€” for they are commensurable in square onlyâ€” and, as AB is to BC, so is the rectangle AB, BC to the square on BC, therefore the rectangle AB, BC is incommensurable with the square on BC. [:ref:`g.343`
]

But twice the rectangle AB, BC is commensurable with the rectangle AB, BC [:ref:`g.338`
], and the squares on AB, BC are commensurable with the square on BCâ€”for AB, BC are rational straight lines commensurable in square onlyâ€” [:ref:`g.349`
] therefore twice the rectangle AB, BC is incommensurable with the squares on AB, BC. [:ref:`g.347`
]

And, componendo, twice the rectangle AB, BC together with the squares on AB, BC, that is, the square on AC [:ref:`g.65`
], is incommensurable with the sum of the squares on AB, BC. [:ref:`g.350`
]

But the sum of the squares on AB, BC is rational; therefore the square on AC is irrational, so that AC is also irrational. [:ref:`g.354`
]

And let it be called binomial.
